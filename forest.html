What is a forest?

A forest is a complex, constantly changing environment made up of a variety of living things, such as trees, shrubs, wildflowers, ferns, mosses, and wildlife. Trees are the dominant feature of a forest. Over 30% of the Earthâ€™s surface is covered with forests.

We live in the temperate deciduous forest biome, which is defined by its four distinct seasons: winter, spring, summer, and fall. In the deciduous forest, the leaves on the trees change color in the fall, lose their leaves at the end of the each growing season, and grow back in the spring. The word deciduous comes from a Latin word that meant to fall down or off.

The animals that live in deciduous forests have also adapted to these yearly changes. Animals adjust to the cold winters by hibernating, migrating, or keeping active all winter.

Why are forests important?

Individually, trees provide shade and relief from the hot sun. Forests are important for the survival of wildlife. Forests also absorb carbon dioxide and replenish the atmosphere with oxygen for us to breathe in a process called photosynthesis. The Deciduous Forest
